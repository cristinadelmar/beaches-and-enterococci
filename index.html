So do you want to go to the beach?

Ten beaches in Puerto Rico are currently not suitable for bathers due to exceeding bacteria levels.

BY CRISTINA DEL MAR QUILES

For Puerto Ricans and many tourists, summer is a synonym of beach time. In the Caribbean archipelago, the coast is the preferred place to sweat the high temperatures.

However, not everything is as paradisiacal as it sounds.

Two weeks ago, journalist Bianca Graulau, shared through her Instagram account a video about nasty waters going into one of the beaches of San Juan. Neighbors have denounced that the hotels in the area frequently discharge used water into the ocean, endangering swimmers, and marine species.

The Puerto Rico Department of Natural and Environmental Resources said that this was just a runoff from the rain. But, as Graulau says, those days, it was not raining.

However, the same agency oversees the Beach Monitoring and Public Notification Program.

Under the Federal Beaches Environmental Assessment and Coastal Health Act of 2000, the department has a duty to identify the level of pathogens in recreational coastal waters that could endanger human health.

In the case of Puerto Rico, the parameter used to evaluate the quality of the water on the beaches is the level of enterococci. If the concentration level of enterococci for a beach exceeds the “Beach Action Value” of 70 colonies per 100 milliliters of water (colonies/100mL) authorities will be required to alert the public about the risk.

The general recommendation is to be alert to the Program notices, since the results may vary from one sample to another.

High levels of enterococcus bacteria can cause diseases of the skin, eyes, nose, throat, and gastrointestinal system, so it is recommended not to bathe on these beaches or do water sports here./

Puerto Rico has more than 270 miles of coastline and more than 300 beaches. So, it is best to look for a beach a little bit farther.

Methodology: I downloaded data from the Puerto Rico Department of Natural and Environmental Resources with information from the Beach Monitoring and Public Notification Program. None of it was in CSV format, so I transformed it and cleaned in Google Sheets an the in Pandas. To see the amount of enterococcus per 100 mL of water in every beach for the last two monitorings, I convert the date to a dateframe and looked for the two most recent dates that each beach was challenged. Then, I made I comparison between the results for those dates to see the variation in contamination.

After having the analysis, I used Datawrapper to show that difference in a graph. Also, I created a map in Datawrapper to show the status of every beach that is part of the program, those that are suitable for swimming and the 10 with a swimming advisory in effect.
